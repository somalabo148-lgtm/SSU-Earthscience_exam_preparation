第1題：生命の多様化と進化の時代

[cite_start]Only toward the end of the Precambrian (approximately 600 to 800 million years ago) do we have any evidence for the presence of more complex, multicellular organisms and, subsequently, of primitive invertebrate life. [cite: 1] [cite_start]The clues are present predominantly in the form of plant impressions, and fossil tracks and traces made by bottom-dwelling marine invertebrates. [cite: 3, 4] [cite_start]However, a rich collection of soft-bodied creatures of perplexing lineage - the so-called Ediacara fauna has been described from the youngest Proterozoic strata (termed "Vendian" in age) of eastern Australia. [cite: 5, 6] [cite_start]By the beginning of the Cambrian period, plants and animals displaying remarkable morphologic diversification and occupying a wide range of habitats burst onto the scene. [cite: 7, 8] [cite_start]To our knowledge, most life forms had developed shells or other preservable hard parts by this time. [cite: 9, 10] [cite_start]Such organisms are more likely to leave fossil evidence than are soft-bodied forms. [cite: 11, 13] [cite_start]Who knows how many floral and faunal types left no record at all? [cite: 14, 15]

[cite_start]The Paleozoic Era, lasting 325 million years, witnessed a proliferation of invertebrates, fishes, and amphibians. [cite: 16, 17] [cite_start]The following 180-million-year-long Mesozoic Era is characterized as the age of dinosaurs. [cite: 18, 19] [cite_start]Reptiles actually arose in the late Paleozoic, and some have survived into the Cenozoic, or age of mammals. [cite: 20, 21] [cite_start]Similarly, primitive mammals existed throughout the Mesozoic Era; however, only after the demise of the dinosaurs approximately 65 million years ago - did they inherit the Earth. [cite: 22, 23] [cite_start]Once the reptiles' reign of terror was largely concluded, mammals rapidly occupied the many ecologic niches previously controlled by their cold-blooded precursors, diversifying and evolving in the process an activity that continues to this day. [cite: 24, 25, 26] [cite_start]The general phenomenon by which organisms evolve in harmony with the changing environment is termed adaptive radiation. [cite: 27, 28]

第2題：始生代の生命と環境（追試）

[cite_start]The evolution of terrestrial life corresponds rather closely to geologic time because traces of biologic activity extend well back into the Archean Eon. [cite: 29, 30, 31] [cite_start]Bacteria that are anaerobic (i.e., that live without consuming oxygen) and photosynthetic have been described from 3.5-billion-year-old siliceous sedimentary deposits of western Australia and must have existed even earlier. [cite: 32, 33, 61] [cite_start]These filamentous single-celled organisms formed domical, or mushroom-like colonies called stromatolites. [cite: 34, 62] [cite_start]They flourished in tidal marine waters, and still do in protected lagoons such as Shark Bay in western Australia, and the Gulf of California. [cite: 35, 63] [cite_start]As fine clay washed over these ancient biogenic mounds and they were buried by later sediments, aqueous fluids percolated through the muds, hardening them into layered rock; [cite: 37, 64] [cite_start]in the process, the original organic structures were replaced by new mineral matter, thus preserving some such colonies as fossil stromatolitic reefs. [cite: 38, 65]

[cite_start]Stromatolites were widespread in the early Earth. [cite: 40, 41, 66] [cite_start]Minerals preserved in Archean strata, as well as computer models of planetary outgassing and paleoclimatology, suggest that early in its history, the planet was enveloped by a strongly reducing atmosphere characterized by the scarcity of free oxygen but rich in methane, hydrogen, and perhaps also ammonia.
